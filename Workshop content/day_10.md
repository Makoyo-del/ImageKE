# Day 10: Formatting vs Content

## Morning Post (Educational)

### LinkedIn Post
That colorful, double-column Canva CV template is the reason you are getting automated rejections.

Many job seekers use graphic design templates with columns, icons, tables, and progress bars to make their CV stand out. They think it looks professional and modern.

ATS software cannot parse complex layouts. They read text from left to right and top to bottom. Columns scramble the reading order, making your experience look like gibberish to the system. If the machine cannot read it, you get rejected.

Use a simple, single-column format. Use standard fonts like Arial, Calibri, or Times New Roman. Avoid text boxes, tables, headers, footers, and progress bars. The simpler the layout, the easier it is for the system to read.

I've reviewed dozens of CVs, and clean, simple black-and-white formats consistently parse with 100% accuracy, while highly designed templates fail.

A CV is a business document, not a design portfolio. You can use AI to build clean, structured content that fits a simple layout.

If this sounds familiar, I'll be teaching the complete workflow inside my upcoming AI Job Seeker Workshop. Seats are limited.

## Afternoon Post (Story / Case Study)

### LinkedIn Post
We changed her resume from a colorful graphic design template to a plain text format. Then she got three callbacks.

She was a talented Marketing Executive, but her CV was a double-column layout with circular skill progress bars (like "Creativity: 80%"). It looked beautiful, but it yielded zero interviews.

The ATS was unable to parse her contact details or job history because they were trapped inside decorative text boxes and sidebars.

We stripped the colors. We moved the content into a clean, single-column layout and replaced the arbitrary progress bars with actual achievement metrics. Within two weeks, she had three interview invites.

One of my clients got hired this week after we switched from a decorative template to an ATS-safe layout.

Your CV needs to be easy to read for the system first, then easy to read for the human recruiter.
